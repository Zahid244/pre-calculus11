Quadratic Functions

Introduction

Quadratic functions model real-world curves like the path of a thrown ball or the shape of a satellite dish. They are defined by a squared term and create a U-shaped graph called a parabola. In this lesson, we'll explore each form of quadratic functions with clear visuals and examples.

1. General Quadratic Form

The most basic form is:

f(x) = a x² + b x + c

Where:

  • a (≠0) controls width and direction
  • b shifts and skews the parabola
  • c is the y-intercept at (0, c)
If a is positive, the parabola opens upward; if negative, downward.

2. Standard Form

This is the general form itself; it's useful for identifying the y-intercept and for applying the quadratic formula:

x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / (2a)
Figure: General/Standard Form Parabola

3. Vertex Form

Vertex form highlights the turning point:

f(x) = a (x - h)² + k
  1. Complete the square on a x² + b x.
  2. Rewrite to find h and k.
Figure: Vertex Form Parabola

4. Factored Form

Factored form reveals the roots (x-intercepts):

f(x) = a (x - r₁) (x - r₂)
  1. Find r₁ and r₂ by solving f(x)=0.
  2. Rewrite the expression.
Figure: Factored Form Parabola

Practice Questions

  1. Convert f(x)=x²+4x+3 into all forms.
  2. Identify vertex and roots for f(x)=2x²-8x+6.
  3. Use the quadratic formula on f(x)=3x²+6x-9.
